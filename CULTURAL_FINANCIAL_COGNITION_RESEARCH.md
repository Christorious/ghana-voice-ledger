# Cultural Financial Cognition Research
## Designing Sales Representation Congruent with Market Women's Financial Thinking

> "We don't need to teach them our way of thinking about money. We need to learn theirs and build technology that speaks their language - both literally and cognitively."

---

## ğŸ¯ Research Objective

**Goal**: Discover and design a sales representation system that is **completely congruent** with how Ghanaian market women naturally think about, remember, and process financial information.

**Why This Matters**: 
- Current digital ledgers impose Western accounting mental models
- This creates cognitive friction and information loss
- Market women have their own sophisticated financial cognition systems
- We should amplify their existing mental models, not replace them

---

## ğŸ” Research Questions

### Primary Questions

1. **How do market women naturally chunk and remember their sales?**
   - By time of day? By customer? By product? By amount?
   - What are the natural "units" of memory?

2. **What financial metrics actually matter to them?**
   - Is it total sales? Profit? Cash in hand? Debt owed?
   - How do they think about "good day" vs "bad day"?

3. **How do they mentally track inventory and pricing?**
   - Visual memory? Physical counting? Spatial reasoning?
   - How do they adjust prices throughout the day?

4. **What are their natural financial rhythms and cycles?**
   - Daily? Weekly? Monthly? Market day cycles?
   - How do they think about time in relation to money?

5. **How do they handle mental math and estimation?**
   - What shortcuts do they use?
   - What's "close enough" vs "needs to be exact"?

6. **What cultural/spiritual beliefs influence their financial thinking?**
   - First sale of the day rituals
   - Lucky numbers or amounts
   - Giving and receiving practices

## ğŸ“Š Proposed Research Methods

### Phase 1: Deep Ethnographic Observation (4-6 weeks)

#### Method: "Shadow & Learn"

**Approach**:
- Spend full market days (6 AM - 6 PM) with 10-15 different vendors
- Don't just interview - **observe** their natural behavior
- Record (with permission) how they:
  - Talk about their sales
  - Remember transactions
  - Count money
  - Make decisions

**Key Observations to Capture**:

1. **Language Patterns**
   - How do they describe a sale? ("I sold 3 tilapia" vs "The tilapia went well today")
   - What metaphors do they use for money/sales?
   - How do they talk about profit vs revenue?

2. **Physical Behaviors**
   - Do they separate money into piles? (morning sales, afternoon sales, etc.)
   - Do they use physical objects as memory aids?
   - How do they organize their products spatially?

3. **Memory Strategies**
   - Do they remember individual customers?
   - Do they remember by product type or by time?
   - What triggers help them recall sales?

4. **Decision Points**
   - When do they check how much they've made?
   - What prompts them to adjust prices?
   - How do they decide when to close for the day?

---

### Phase 2: Cognitive Mapping Interviews (2-3 weeks)

#### Method: "Think-Aloud Protocol"

**Approach**: Ask vendors to reconstruct their day while thinking aloud

**Sample Questions**:

1. **End of Day Reconstruction**
   - "Tell me about your day. How did it go?"
   - "How do you know if you had a good day?"
   - "Can you remember your sales? How do you remember them?"

2. **Mental Organization**
   - "When you think about your sales, what comes to mind first?"
   - "How do you keep track in your head?"
   - "What helps you remember?"

3. **Financial Goals**
   - "What number are you trying to reach each day?"
   - "How do you know when you've reached it?"
   - "What do you do with the money you make?"

4. **Information Needs**
   - "What do you wish you knew about your sales?"
   - "What information would help you make better decisions?"
   - "What's hard to keep track of?"

---

### Phase 3: Participatory Design Sessions (3-4 weeks)

#### Method: "Co-Creation Workshops"

**Approach**: Work WITH market women to design the representation

**Activities**:

1. **Card Sorting Exercise**
   - Give them cards with different ways to show sales data
   - Ask them to organize by "makes sense" vs "confusing"
   - Discover their natural categorization

2. **Sketch Your Ideal View**
   - Provide paper, markers, stickers
   - Ask: "If you could see your sales any way you want, what would it look like?"
   - Don't constrain to digital - let them dream

3. **Story-Based Scenarios**
   - "Imagine you want to know if today was better than yesterday..."
   - "Imagine you want to see which fish sells best..."
   - How would they want to see this information?

4. **Physical Prototyping**
   - Use physical objects (coins, stones, cards) to represent sales
   - Let them arrange and rearrange
   - Observe their natural organizational patterns

## ğŸ§  Hypotheses to Test

Based on preliminary understanding of Ghanaian market culture:

### Hypothesis 1: Time-Based Chunking
**Theory**: Market women may naturally chunk their day into meaningful periods rather than continuous time.

**Examples**:
- "Morning rush" (6-9 AM)
- "Slow time" (9 AM-12 PM)
- "Lunch rush" (12-2 PM)
- "Evening" (2-6 PM)

**Test**: Do they remember sales by these periods rather than by clock time?

**Design Implication**: Show sales grouped by natural market rhythms, not hourly charts.

---

### Hypothesis 2: Relational Memory
**Theory**: Sales may be remembered in relation to people and stories, not just numbers.

**Examples**:
- "The woman who always buys 2 tilapia"
- "The man who negotiated hard today"
- "The customer who paid with exact change"

**Test**: Do they recall transactions by customer relationships?

**Design Implication**: Show sales with customer context, not just amounts.

---

### Hypothesis 3: Physical-Spatial Cognition
**Theory**: Market women may use spatial/physical mental models for tracking.

**Examples**:
- Visualizing money in piles
- Remembering products by their physical location
- Using body/hand gestures to indicate amounts

**Test**: Do they use spatial metaphors when describing sales?

**Design Implication**: Use spatial/visual representations, not just numbers and lists.

---

### Hypothesis 4: Comparative Rather Than Absolute
**Theory**: "Better than yesterday" may matter more than exact amounts.

**Examples**:
- "Today was slower than yesterday"
- "This week is better than last week"
- "Tilapia is selling better than mackerel"

**Test**: Do they think in comparisons rather than absolutes?

**Design Implication**: Emphasize trends and comparisons over exact numbers.

---

### Hypothesis 5: Goal-Oriented Tracking
**Theory**: Progress toward a daily/weekly goal may be more meaningful than total amount.

**Examples**:
- "I need 500 cedis today to pay school fees"
- "I'm halfway to my goal"
- "Just 100 more cedis and I'm done"

**Test**: Do they have specific targets they're working toward?

**Design Implication**: Show progress bars and goal completion, not just totals.

---

### Hypothesis 6: Profit-First Thinking
**Theory**: They may think about profit (what they keep) before revenue (what they sell).

**Examples**:
- "I made 200 cedis today" (meaning profit, not sales)
- Mentally subtracting costs automatically
- Thinking about "what I can take home"

**Test**: When they say "I made X," do they mean revenue or profit?

**Design Implication**: Show profit prominently, not just sales totals.

## ğŸ› ï¸ Implementation Strategy

### Step 1: Research First (Don't Assume)
- Conduct all three research phases
- Document findings rigorously
- Challenge our assumptions

### Step 2: Synthesize Insights
- Identify common patterns across vendors
- Find the "mental models" they actually use
- Map their cognitive strategies

### Step 3: Design Multiple Prototypes
- Create 5-7 different representation concepts
- Base each on a different cognitive model discovered
- Make them radically different from each other

### Step 4: Test with Real Users
- Show prototypes to market women
- Ask: "Which makes sense to you?"
- Observe which they naturally gravitate toward

### Step 5: Iterate and Refine
- Combine best elements
- Remove what confuses
- Add what's missing

### Step 6: Build and Deploy
- Implement the winning design(s)
- Allow users to switch between views
- Continue learning and adapting

## ğŸ’¡ Key Principles

### 2. **Respect**

### 3. **Co-Creation**

### 4. **Flexibility**

Financial thinking is deeply cultural. Honor that.

The best design is the one they actually use and find helpful.
